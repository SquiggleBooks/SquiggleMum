It’s been a long time coming, but with two school aged kids and a busy life offline – I’m finally ready to let go of SquiggleMum. Well, the mum part anyway. 

I rarely write about parenting these days, and I feel that stories of my children’s adventures (or misadventures as the case may be) aren’t for me to share anymore. So I’m making a new home online, at squigglebooks.com

Over at SquiggleBooks you’ll find a mix of book reviews, posts about children’s literacy, thoughts about education, and tips for teachers. You’ll also be able to keep up with the latest news about my own writing career, and definitely be among the first to know if when I land that elusive picture book contract. It’s a pretty basic site for now, but I expect it will grow and change just as this blog has.

Before I go, I’d like to thank a bunch of people who have been part of the SquiggleMum journey. There have been thousands of comments on the blog over the years – thank you to each of you who took time to connect here. Some readers have become friends – thank you for staying in touch and encouraging me along the way. I have been privileged to write for a number of other parenting sites and share parenting advice on air – thank you for having me.  I have also won several awards and been invited to speak about blogging at conferences – thank you for the opportunity. Thank you to the bloggers who have been with me since the beginning – especially Christie from Childhood101, as well as Kate from Picklebums, and Kelly from Be A Fun Mum. (There are too many others to name!)

I hope that many of you will join me in this next chapter…

Blessings,

Cath / SquiggleMum.

“One kiss for baby, under mistletoe. Two kisses for baby, catching falling snow…”

Title: Count My Christmas Kisses

Publisher: Harper Collins / ABC Books

Author: Ruthie May

Illustrator: Tamsin Ainslie

Comment: A sweet, simple counting book perfect for baby’s first Christmas. It’s unfortunately very European for an Australian picture book (no snow here!) but it is delightful nonetheless. Recommended for Under 2s, and for young children who have welcomed a baby sibling this year.

 

“With the fire crackling in the background, they all helped with the mixture for Mr Darcy’s famous Christmas pudding…”

Title: Mr Darcy and the Christmas Pudding

Publisher: New Frontier

Author: Alex Field

Illustrator: Peter Carnavas

Comment: Alex Field has previously released two other Mr Darcy books, also illustrated by Peter Carnavas. This one incorporates the tradition of Stir Up Sunday, and a brief factual account of the origin of the tradition is included at the end. For fans of Mr Darcy – the duck. Recommended for ages 5-8.

 

“His bauble bombs smell so bad I can’t wrap presents in my room…”

Title: There is a Monster Under My Christmas Tree Who Farts

Publisher: Harper Collins / ABC Books

Author/Illustrator: Tim Miller and Matt Stanton

Comment: I cannot believe I just typed that title on my blog. Sigh. Ok, ok, so it’s not my style – but it is bound to appeal to some kids this Christmas. And to Uncles. Lots and lots of uncles. Recommended for Uncles aged 20-50.

 

Other highly recommended new releases to look out for:

  • One Night (Penny Matthews / Stephen Michael King). This tells the story of the first Christmas through the eyes of the animals and is my pick for 2014.
  • Tea and Sugar Christmas (Jane Jolly / Robert Ingpen). Based on the true story of the train that travelled across the Nullabor Plain weekly for over 80 years. Features critically acclaimed Australian illustrator Robert Ingpen.
  • Little Dog and the Christmas Wish (Corinne Fenton / Robin Cowcher). The illustrations are quintessentially Melbourne, and Corinne Fenton’s stories are always poetically told. For dog lovers.

Author: Jodie Wells-Slowgrove

Illustrator: Kerry Millard

Publisher: Penguin Australia

SquiggleKid’s comments (aged7):

I’ve read lots of Rainbow Magic Fairies books, so when I heard about the Wilderness Fairies I was very interested. I was hoping that these books by Jodie Wells-Slowgrove would be a bit more interesting (because the Rainbow Magic books are ALL the same)!

I was right. The Wilderness Fairies books are much more interesting and I really enjoyed them. In the first book, Daisy’s Quest, we meet Daisy and a lot of other Australian creatures that help her on her quest to find her wings. In the second book, Daisy learns how to use them. Wings are more troublesome than she expects.

I found these books easy to read. The pictures helped me to understand what was going on. I really liked the setting because I know about the Australian bush. I think 7-9 year olds will enjoy the series. There are some great colouring pages at the Wilderness Fairies website. I can’t wait for books three and four!

Recommended for:

  • kids who like the Rainbow Magic series
  • easy chapter books
  • discussing the Australian bush

Author: Corinne Fenton

Illustrator: Hannah Sommerville

Publisher: Ford St Publishing

SquiggleMum’s comments:

Chasing Shadows is a story about finding a way forward through grief and loss. This is a poignant picture book – beautifully told, and heart breakingly sad.

Beth is overwhelmed by emotions and sees only shadows where her mother once was. She isn’t ready for the puppy her father brings home, but perhaps the puppy can help in ways her father can’t.

Chasing Shadows isn’t necessarily a book for the classroom, however it will be a useful resource for those who work with children and families. Few books deal with grief through the eyes of a child, and Fenton’s expertise as a storyteller shines through. Sommerville’s sensitive illustrations give voice to Beth’s unspoken feelings, and the shadows show what Beth misses most about her mother.

Recommended for:

  • Families experiencing loss
  • Teachers of children who are grieving
  • Psychologists
  • Exploration and discussion of feelings

If you haven’t come across them yet, let me introduce you to That’s My Story cards.

The tin is about the size of your phone and contains a set of glossy story telling cards. Each card has a simple isolated image on a white background.

  • Red cards = character or object
  • Green cards = setting
  • Yellow cards = complication
  • Blue card = conclusion

Every good story contains these elements, from picture books through to novels. Kids who recognise the basic elements of a story and understand narrative structure usually do much better in formal literacy lessons once they start school.

You can check out the suggested game ideas on the website, but I would keep it simple. “Once upon a time there was a… (red card + green card). Suddenly… (yellow card). Oh no! But then…(happy ending).” 

I think these open ended cards will get a good workout in our family: in the car, at coffee shops, at bedtime. They are also available as foam stickers for bath time story telling! They retail for $16.95 and would make excellent stocking stuffers, or Christmas gifts for little friends.

SquiggleMum’s comments:

Bailey feels blah. He doesn’t like school, doesn’t have any friends, and as a result – his tummy doesn’t feel so good either. But when Bailey befriends the new kid at school, things start to look up.

This is a very useful book for discussing issues around emotional health and wellness, including sadness, depression, resilience, and coping strategies. It is not a book that will get the “read-it-again” response from most children, but I’ve given it 4 stars because of its potential for practical application. Bailey Beats The Blah is aligned with Kids Matter, national education curriculum and supported by Kids Help Line.

Bailey Beats the Blah is available from Amazon and KarenTyrrell.com.  Teacher’s notes and student activities are also available.

Recommended for:

  • Lower Primary
  • Building emotional resilience
  • Discussing depression
  • Role play

Author: Michelle Worthington

Illustrator: Ann-Marie Finn

Publisher: Wybble

SquiggleMum’s comments:

This gentle recount tells of a little girl’s day at the beach, from sunrise to sunset. The text is in present tense, giving the story a sense of “now” and helping young readers to enter the narrative. Finn’s warm illustrations complement the soft text.

“Start of the Ocean” is a nice example of independent publishing, although an editor’s touch is missing from the book overall. However, this doesn’t stop it being a sweet tale which families will enjoy reading together. It is definitely suited more to the home environment than the classroom.

Recommended for:

  • Early childhood
  • Exploring simple sentence structure in present tense (I am standing, I am climbing, I am playing, I am swimming…) 
  • Talking about the beach (what can you see? hear? taste? touch? smell?)
  • Beach holiday bedtime stories

Rating:
